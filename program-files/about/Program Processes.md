# Program Processes

## Overview

Automation program has the following processes to achieve its goals.

1. Strategy
2. Develop
3. Org Dev

## Processes

### Strategy

Strategy process works on Objectives, and goes through the following stages:

1. **Intention** — A strategic objective is proposed by the Program Manager or a steering stakeholder based on identified needs or opportunities. It captures the desired outcome at a high level but is not yet scoped or committed to.
2. **Develop** — The objective is scoped, key results are defined with measurable targets, and dependencies across programs are identified. Alignment is sought with stakeholders, ending with a go/no-go decision to commit.
3. **Active** — The objective is aligned, committed, and published to the operational roadmap. Linked work items are created on program boards, progress is tracked through regular syncs, and status is reported in the Tech Leaders meeting.
4. **Done** — The objective is no longer actively being worked on; key results have been fully or partially achieved with a documented outcome. Linked work items should be in Done or closed status.
5. **Archived** — The objective is retired from the active view and preserved for historical reference. No further status updates or work items are expected.

The [AUTO project timeline](https://utinternational.jira.com/jira/core/projects/AUTO/timeline?rangeMode=quarters) is used to track the process.

### Develop

Process to develop an **Idea** to a business **Impact**.

> *(Diagram in the source Confluence page illustrates the Develop process flow.)*

The following describe the different stages in the program Develop process:

1. **Idea** — Capture and intake of new ideas, intentions, and signals from stakeholders and data sources. Ideas are logged without evaluation to preserve a broad pipeline of possibilities.
2. **Opportunity** — Evaluate captured ideas for commercial and technical viability, and develop qualified ideas into concrete business opportunities. Each opportunity is assessed for strategic alignment, feasibility, and expected value.
3. **Analysis** — Define the problem scope, desired outcomes, and solution approach. This stage produces the solution design, success criteria, and a realization decision to proceed to planning.
4. **Planning** — Determine the resources, dependencies, and delivery timeline required for implementation. The output is a committed plan with milestones and accountable owners.
5. **Implementation** — Execute the plan through iterative design, build, testing, and staged roll-out. Progress is tracked against milestones, and adjustments are made as needed.
6. **Review** — Assess delivered outcomes against the defined success criteria. Identify gaps, capture lessons learned, and apply refinements to close the impact delta.
7. **Done** — The intended impact has been achieved and validated. The work item is closed and its outcomes are handed over to operations or steady-state ownership.
8. **Archived** — Completed or discontinued items are archived for historical reference. No further action is expected.

The different [program work boards](https://utinternational.jira.com/wiki/spaces/AUTO/pages/4617437204) are used to track the work items in the Develop process.

There are 3 types of work items that flow through the Develop process, plus 1 grouping mechanism:

1. **Story** — A discrete feature or enhancement to be built into an application. Stories flow through all stages of the Develop process, from Idea through Review, and produce a deployable deliverable.
2. **Study** — An analytical effort to explore an idea or opportunity and produce a recommendation or decision. Studies typically conclude at Analysis and may not proceed through Planning, Implementation, or Review.
3. **Task** — A general-purpose work item for data operations, maintenance, or other activities that don't qualify as a Story or Study. Tasks follow the Develop process stages as applicable to their scope.
4. **Workstream** — A grouping container that bundles multiple related work items under a shared purpose or objective. Workstreams do not flow through the process themselves but provide traceability across the Stories, Studies, and Tasks within them.

### Org Dev

Organization Development (shorten as Org Dev) is a supporting process that prepares people, roles, and workflows to adopt and sustain changes introduced by an automation initiative. It applies when the scope of transformation extends beyond technical delivery and requires structured change management — such as stakeholder alignment, training, communication, or role redesign — best led within the Automation program itself.

This process is applied on a case-by-case basis and is currently in its early stages. Its structure and methods will evolve as the program matures its change management practice.

## Roles

The following are key roles in the processes above.

### Program Manager

1. Define and maintain the program roadmap, ensuring objectives are aligned with the department's strategic priorities and sequenced for maximum impact.
2. Evaluate and approve new ideas and opportunities for inclusion in the program, balancing value, feasibility, and resource capacity.
3. Monitor overall program health across all objectives, escalate cross-cutting risks, and make trade-off decisions on scope, timeline, and resources.
4. Facilitate regular syncs with stakeholders and technology leaders, ensuring visibility into progress, blockers, and upcoming commitments.
5. Establish and evolve the program's processes, standards, and tooling to improve how the team works across the Develop and Org Dev lifecycles.

### Objective Lead

1. Establish and maintain the breakdown of milestones, ownership, and schedule of resources required to achieve the objective's intended impact.
2. Ensure consistent forward progress towards impact, identify obstacles early, and arrange to overcome them through coordination or escalation.
3. Monitor progress with regards to timely impact and efforts spent, and post updates in the roadmap board.
4. Align with the Program Manager on scope, priority shifts, and dependencies that affect the objective's timeline or expected outcomes.
5. Facilitate collaboration across functional teams and stakeholders within the objective, ensuring shared understanding of goals, decisions, and status.

### Development Lead

1. Establish and maintain the breakdown of tasks, ownership, and schedule of resources required to complete the assigned work item.
2. Ensure consistent forward progress towards completion, identify obstacles early, and arrange to overcome them through coordination or escalation to the Objective Lead or Program Manager.
3. Monitor progress with regards to timely delivery and efforts spent, and post updates in the program work board.
